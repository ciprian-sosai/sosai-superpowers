# Receiving Peer Review

## Overview

Not all feedback is correct. Accepting every suggestion without evaluation produces work that pleases the reviewer but may not serve the goal.

**Rule:** Understand before implementing. Question before accepting.

## For Each Piece of Feedback

- [ ] Do you understand what the reviewer is saying?
- [ ] Is the feedback based on the actual work or a misunderstanding?
- [ ] Does implementing it move the work closer to the stated goal?
- [ ] If you disagree: can you explain why clearly?

## Response Options

| Situation | Response |
|---|---|
| Feedback is valid and clear | Implement it |
| Feedback is valid but unclear | Ask for clarification before implementing |
| Feedback conflicts with requirements | Flag the conflict, ask how to resolve |
| Feedback is based on a misunderstanding | Explain the original intent |
| Feedback is a matter of preference | Note it, decide deliberately |

## What Not To Do

- Do not implement feedback you don't understand
- Do not reject feedback without being able to articulate why
- Do not agree just to avoid conflict
- Do not implement everything and hope it works out

## Examples

**Example 1: Marketing campaign copy review**
User: "My manager reviewed my campaign brief and wants me to change the target audience segment entirely."
Applied: The skill prompts evaluation of whether the suggestion is based on the actual brief or a misunderstanding, and whether it conflicts with the approved campaign strategy.
Result: You flag the conflict with the original strategy to your manager before making changes, rather than silently overwriting your work.

**Example 2: Finance report feedback**
User: "A colleague reviewed my quarterly variance report and says the commentary is too detailed."
Applied: The skill surfaces whether this is a preference or a real requirement, and who the intended audience is.
Result: You check the report distribution list and audience expectations, then decide deliberately rather than trimming based on one opinion.

**Example 3: Legal contract redline review**
User: "Outside counsel sent back 12 redlines on the vendor agreement. Some I don't understand."
Applied: The skill blocks implementation of unclear feedback and prompts you to request clarification on each item you cannot explain in your own words.
Result: You send a targeted reply asking counsel to explain three specific redlines before accepting them, avoiding accidental scope changes.

## Troubleshooting

**You implement feedback first, then evaluate it after**
Stop. The skill only works before implementation. Revert the change, apply the evaluation checklist, then decide whether to re-implement.

**Reviewer has authority over you and pushback feels risky**
Disagreement is not refusal. Use the "flag the conflict" response — frame it as a clarifying question, not a rejection.

**Multiple reviewers gave contradictory feedback**
Do not average or ignore. Treat each conflict as a flagged item requiring explicit resolution before you proceed on either suggestion.

**You agreed with all feedback in the moment and later regret it**
If you cannot articulate why a piece of feedback improves the work, that is a signal you agreed to avoid conflict — the rule not to do this was broken.

**Feedback is vague ("make it tighter", "sharpen the argument") and you implement a guess**
Vague feedback belongs in the "ask for clarification" row. Do not interpret and implement — ask the reviewer what specific change they have in mind.
